ORIGINAL EMAIL Subject: Vanderbilt connection, quick intro on private credit platform
Hi Amy, I hope you're doing well. I'm reaching out as a fellow Vanderbilt alum. I'm currently with On Pepper, where we've built Pepper, a platform purpose-built for private credit. Developed by a former Blackstone professional, it's designed to address the operational complexity that comes with managing and scaling credit portfolios at firms like Antares Capital. Across the market, we consistently see teams managing origination, portfolio monitoring, and reporting across multiple systems and spreadsheets. Pepper brings the full investment lifecycle into a single platform, with a focus on: - Driving efficiency through AI and automation, reducing manual workload - Improving data integrity and controls across assets and fund structures - Enhancing portfolio monitoring (covenants, KPIs, early warning signals) - Providing real-time visibility to better support investment decisions and LP reporting Given your role and perspective in private credit, I thought this might be relevant. Would you be open to a brief 20-minute introduction in the coming days? I'd value the opportunity to share what we're seeing across the market and get your perspective. Regards, Eric
Problems identified
01
The body reads like a product brochure, not a pain diagnosis
Critical
The four bullet points describe what Pepper does in Pepper's language. Amy does not wake up thinking "I need to improve data integrity across fund structures." She thinks about how many hours her team spent rebuilding last month's LP report across three spreadsheets and whether the numbers even match. The email speaks from inside the product, looking out. It needs to speak from inside Amy's Tuesday morning, looking in.
Fix
Remove the bullet points entirely. Replace with one sentence naming Amy's specific operational pain in language she uses internally, then position Pepper as the answer to that named pain.
02
The Blackstone credential is buried in paragraph two
Critical
"Developed by a former Blackstone professional" is the most powerful sentence in this email. For an audience of private credit professionals, Blackstone is the highest institutional trust signal in their world. It is sitting in the middle of paragraph two, competing with feature copy. Most busy professionals do not read that far before deciding whether to reply.
Fix
Move the Blackstone line to the first or second sentence of the email, every time, for every prospect in this space. It should be the second thing they read, right after the relationship hook.
03
Personalisation drops off after the opening line
High
Antares Capital is named once in a subordinate clause, then the rest of the email becomes generic. "Across the market, we consistently see teams..." could have been pasted into an email to 300 people. Amy would sense this even if she could not articulate why. That realisation weakens the trust the alumni connection started to build.
Fix
One additional specific detail about Antares, their AUM, their portfolio complexity, or a publicly known operational reality at their scale changes how the entire email reads. Personalisation is not just a name in the greeting. It is a detail that proves you actually looked.
04
The CTA explains what you get from the call, not what Amy gets
High
"I'd value the opportunity to share what we're seeing and get your perspective" is your motivation. Amy's implicit question before agreeing to any meeting is: what do I walk away with from 20 minutes of my time? That question is not answered. For a senior professional in private credit, time is the most protected resource they have.
Fix
Reframe the CTA as an exchange, not a request. "I can share what we're seeing operationally across private credit teams at your scale, useful context whether or not Pepper ends up being the right fit" makes the call feel like peer intelligence, not a vendor demo.
Why the rewrite works
1
Blackstone lands in line two where it does actual credibility work instead of getting buried in feature copy
2
One sentence replaces four bullet points and names Amy's pain in her language, not Pepper's feature vocabulary
3
The CTA frames the call as an intelligence exchange so Amy has a reason to say yes, not just you
4
Under 120 words total. Shorter signals confidence. Longer signals uncertainty about whether one strong idea is enough.

ORIGINAL EMAIL Subject: Reconnecting after JPMorgan
Hi Beth, I hope you've been doing well, it's been a long time since our JPMorgan days, and I've been meaning to reach out. I'd love to catch up and hear what you've been up to since then. If you happen to be in New York at any point, it would be great to grab a coffee. Otherwise, a quick call would be just as good. Let me know what works for you, I'd really enjoy reconnecting. Regards, Eric
Problems identified
01
Personal tone sent from a corporate CRO email creates a hidden agenda feeling
Critical
Beth receives an email that reads entirely social but arrives from eric.mueller@onpepper.com with a full company signature, copyright footer, and CRO title. The gap between the warm personal body copy and the clearly commercial sender context creates confusion about the purpose. Beth cannot tell if this is a genuine personal reconnect or the warmup before a pitch. That ambiguity makes it easy to postpone replying indefinitely. She will mean to respond and never get around to it.
Fix
If the intent is purely social, send from a personal email. If there is a business reason behind the reconnect, name it briefly and without apology at the end. Transparency about purpose is more respectful of the relationship than ambiguity.
02
No reason to reply today versus in two weeks
Critical
"If you happen to be in New York at any point" is the softest possible CTA. There is no hook, no timely trigger, nothing that makes replying today feel different from replying someday. In a busy professional's inbox, emails with open timelines get filed under "when I have a moment" and that moment never comes.
Fix
Give the reconnect a natural, timely hook tied to something real at her firm or in her market. This gives Beth a reason to respond now rather than eventually.
03
Valuable relationship capital is spent with nothing to show for it commercially
High
Beth is a warm contact with a real shared history. Using that relationship capital entirely on a social reconnect with no business bridge means if you want to bring Pepper into the conversation later, you will need to start that conversation cold. One brief, transparent line acknowledging there is something business-relevant to share gives Beth a choice. Right now she is only given one option and it does not move Pepper forward.
Fix
Add one sentence at the close: "Also building something in the private credit space I think you would find interesting, would love your perspective when we connect." Honest, not pushy, and it opens the door.
Why the rewrite works
1
The warm personal tone is fully preserved. Nothing about the rewrite feels salesy or forced.
2
A specific timely hook tied to something real at her firm gives Beth a reason to reply now rather than someday
3
One transparent line about Pepper lets Beth opt into the business conversation from a position of choice, not ambush
4
The commercial intent is acknowledged without dominating the email. The reconnect stays the primary purpose.

Would love to catch up. 20 minutes by phone if you are open, or coffee if you are ever in New York. For secondaries investors, Pepper centralizes underlying fund and position data, streamlines the tracking of cash flows and valuations across numerous GP relationships, and provides real-time visibility into exposures, liquidity, and performance. Problems identified
01
The referral is name-dropped and then abandoned
Critical
Michael Kaufman's name appears in the opening sentence and disappears entirely. A warm referral is the highest trust signal in cold outreach to institutional buyers. It should be framing the whole email, not sitting in one sentence. As written, Gerry cannot sense whether this is a genuine referral or a fabricated one, because nothing in the rest of the email connects back to what Michael said or why he thought of Gerry specifically.
Fix
Lean into the referral as the frame for the whole email. Quote or paraphrase what Michael said and why he thought of Gerry specifically. "Michael mentioned specifically that the operational complexity at Newbury's scale was worth a conversation" makes the referral feel real and targeted, not generic.
02
The body is written in Pepper's language, not Gerry's
Critical
"Eliminates the operational risk and inefficiency of spreadsheet-based processes." "Scalable, institutional-grade approach." "Centralizes underlying fund and position data." These are Pepper's words. A PE secondaries PM at Newbury thinks in terms of GP relationship management, IRR tracking across hundreds of positions, and LP reporting timelines that require pulling data from sources that do not naturally talk to each other. The email describes a solution using the solution's vocabulary rather than the buyer's.
Fix
Replace one full paragraph of Pepper feature copy with one sentence naming Gerry's specific operational pain in his language. "Managing cash flow tracking and valuations across dozens of GP relationships creates a reconciliation burden that grows faster than headcount" is Gerry's world, not a product description.
03
A warm referral email should be shorter than a cold email, not longer
High
With a warm referral, 70% of the credibility is already in place before Gerry reads a single word. The email does not need to sell the product. It needs to earn the meeting. The current length, four substantial paragraphs, signals uncertainty that the referral alone is enough to get a reply. For an institutional buyer, a long first email from a vendor reads as overselling.
Fix
Three short paragraphs maximum. Referral context and Blackstone credential. One sentence of named pain. CTA framed as an exchange. The product details belong in the call, not the intro email.
04
The CTA is framed around what you get, not what Gerry gets
High
"I'd love to connect and share a bit more about what we're doing" is your agenda stated plainly. A secondaries PM managing a multi-billion dollar portfolio needs to know what he personally gets from 20 minutes of his time. Without that answer, the calculation defaults to: this benefits you more than it benefits me.
Fix
Reframe the CTA as intelligence sharing, not a product intro. "I can share what we're seeing operationally across secondaries teams at your scale, whether or not Pepper is the right fit, it's usually a useful 20 minutes" makes the call feel like a peer conversation, not a sales demo request.
Why the rewrite works
1
The referral frames the whole email. Gerry senses that Michael actually said something specific about him, which makes this feel genuine rather than a name-drop.
2
Blackstone lands in paragraph one where it earns credibility before any feature copy appears
3
One sentence of pain in Gerry's language replaces two paragraphs of feature description. Less is doing more work.
4
The CTA gives Gerry a reason to take the call that is about his benefit, not yours

The common thread
One pattern runs through all three emails.

The individual fixes matter. But the reason all three emails share the same conversion problem is one structural habit. Identify this and fixing any future email becomes straightforward.

1
Every email is written from inside Pepper, looking out
The most effective B2B email to an institutional buyer names their specific operational pain in their language before saying a single word about the product. The emails as written prove Pepper is good. They do not prove you understand what the reader deals with on a Tuesday morning. That gap is where the replies go.
2
The Blackstone credential is not being used as the anchor it is
For alternative asset managers, "built by a former Blackstone professional" is the single most credibility-efficient sentence available. It signals peer-level understanding, institutional pedigree, and product depth in six words. In every email it appears either buried or missing. It should be in the first two sentences of every outreach to this audience, every time.
3
Every CTA is framed as a request, not an exchange
Every meeting ask in these three emails explains what you or Pepper gets from the call. The prospect's reason to say yes goes unanswered. Reframing every CTA as an intelligence exchange rather than a demo request is a single change that typically doubles reply rates with this buyer type.
